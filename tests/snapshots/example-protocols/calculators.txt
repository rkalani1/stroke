NIHSS Scoring Pitfalls
1a (LOC):
Do NOT score sedation/intubation as decreased LOC. Score best response. If patient requires stimulation only because of intubation/sedation, score 0.
3 (Visual):
Must use finger counting or visual stimulus, NOT visual threat. Test each eye's temporal and nasal field.
5/6 (Motor):
"Drift" = limb falls before 10s (arms) or 5s (legs). Amputation/fusion = 0, not UN. Test arms with palms down.
7 (Ataxia):
Test ONLY if there is no weakness obscuring assessment. If limb is plegic, score 0 (not UN). Ataxia must be out of proportion to weakness.
9 (Language):
Requires testing naming, reading, AND description — comprehension alone is insufficient. Use standardized picture card.
11 (Extinction):
Must test BILATERAL SIMULTANEOUS visual AND tactile stimulation, not just unilateral.
Posterior circulation bias:
Scale heavily weighted toward anterior/left MCA. Devastating posterior strokes (diplopia, vertigo, ataxia, dysphagia) may score 0-3. NIHSS 0 does NOT exclude stroke.
General rule:
Score WORST performance during exam, not best. Do not coach the patient.
NIHSS Exam Technique Guide (for telestroke coaching)
Use this to coach spoke ED staff through each NIHSS item over video/phone.
1a. LOC:
Observe alertness. If not alert, apply escalating stimuli: voice → loud voice → nail-bed pressure. Score response to each level. Do not attribute decreased LOC to sedation or intubation.
1b. LOC Questions:
Ask: "What month is it?" and "What is your age?" Patient must answer both correctly (no partial credit). Intubated/dysarthric patients who approximate answers score 1. Do NOT accept pointing or gestures.
1c. LOC Commands:
"Open and close your eyes" then "Grip and release your non-paretic hand." If patient cannot use hands, substitute another one-step command. Score only first attempt. Demonstrate if there is a language barrier.
2. Best Gaze:
Test horizontal eye movements only. Ask patient to look left and right, or track your finger. If patient cannot follow commands, use oculocephalic reflex (doll's eyes). Score only conjugate horizontal gaze deviation or gaze palsy.
3. Visual Fields:
Test by finger counting or visual stimulus in all four quadrants. Patient fixates on your nose. Present 1-3 fingers in upper and lower temporal and nasal fields of each eye separately, or simultaneously if bilateral assessment needed. Do NOT use visual threat (finger poke) — it tests a different pathway. If one eye blind, test the other.
4. Facial Palsy:
Ask patient to show teeth (or smile) AND raise eyebrows. Compare both sides. If patient cannot follow commands: observe symmetry of grimace to nail-bed pressure. Central palsy = lower face only; peripheral = upper and lower face.
5a/5b. Motor Arms:
Position: arms extended 90° if sitting, 45° if supine, palms DOWN. Count aloud to 10. Score each arm separately. "Drift" = any downward movement before 10 seconds. If limb cannot be positioned against gravity due to pain/contracture, score best effort. Amputation/fusion = 0.
6a/6b. Motor Legs:
Position: supine, each leg raised to 30° from bed. Count aloud to 5. Score each leg separately. "Drift" = leg falls to bed before 5 seconds. Test one leg at a time.
7. Limb Ataxia:
Finger-nose-finger test (arms) and heel-shin test (legs). Patient must reach YOUR finger, not their own nose repeatedly. Test must be meaningful — do NOT test if limb is plegic (score 0) or if weakness obscures assessment. Ataxia must be OUT OF PROPORTION to weakness. Eyes open during testing.
8. Sensory:
Use pinprick (broken tongue depressor or pin). Test face, arm, trunk, and leg bilaterally. Ask "Does this feel sharp? Does it feel the same on both sides?" If patient cannot report, score grimace asymmetry to noxious stimulus. Only stroke-related loss counts — pre-existing neuropathy = 0.
9. Best Language:
Three required components: (1) Show cookie theft picture — patient describes the scene; (2) Naming card — patient names objects (feather, hammock, cactus); (3) Reading card — patient reads sentences aloud. Comprehension alone is NOT sufficient. If no cards available: ask patient to describe what happened, name objects in the room, read text on a clipboard. Mute/intubated who cannot write = score 3.
10. Dysarthria:
Ask patient to read or repeat: "You know how." "Down to earth." "I got home from work." "Near the table in the dining room." "They heard him speak on the radio last night." Score clarity. Intubated/mechanical barrier = UN. If patient has severe aphasia, score dysarthria from spontaneous speech.
11. Extinction/Inattention:
Two simultaneous tests required: (1) VISUAL: Stand in front of patient, raise one finger on each side in peripheral fields simultaneously — ask "Which side did you see?" (2) TACTILE: With patient's eyes closed, touch both hands simultaneously — ask "Where did I touch you?" Both must be tested. Score only if one-sided extinction (perceives single stimuli but ignores one side during bilateral stimulation).

PASCAL Classification (Kent et al., JAHA 2020)
Definite PFO-related stroke (closure strongly recommended):
RoPE ≥7 + high-risk PFO (large shunt or atrial septal aneurysm)
Probable PFO-related stroke (closure reasonable):
• RoPE ≥7 + low-risk PFO (small shunt, no ASA)
• RoPE <7 + high-risk PFO (large shunt or ASA)
Possible PFO-related stroke (closure uncertain benefit):
RoPE <7 + low-risk PFO, no clear alternative stroke etiology
Unlikely PFO-related stroke (closure not recommended):
Clear alternative stroke etiology identified (AF, LAA, etc.), regardless of PFO features
