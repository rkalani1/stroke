There is a paucity of evidence informing the treatment of acute ischemic stroke in childhood, and thus, we cannot provide formal recommendations by telephone. We can help to confirm stroke, evaluate contraindications to acute reperfusion therapy, and provide a framework for shared medical decision making with patients and their families. An MRI is the optimal imaging modality, but CT might be sufficient if acute ischemic stroke is strongly suspected based on clinical history and examination findings.

Formal recommendations are available from the American Heart Association. # Acute Thrombolytics in Children:

Neither tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) nor Tenecteplase (TNK) are FDA approved for childhood acute ischemic stroke. There is a paucity of clinical evidence supporting the safety and efficacy of thrombolytic therapy in children; guidance is largely provided by the prematurely terminated TIPS study, case reports, and small clinical cohort studies. Acute thrombolytic therapy with TNK is reasonable to administer selectively in children with acute stroke, based upon expert opinion recommendations and extrapolation of data from adult studies (1-9).

Dose of TNK for acute stroke in children (same as adult dose): 0.25mg/kg (maximum dose 25mg).

Dose of tPA for acute stroke in children (same as adult dose): 0.9mg/kg (maximum dose of 90mg); given as an initial 10% IV bolus over 1-minute followed by 90% of the dose as an infusion over 60 minutes.

Contraindications for TNK/tPA in children:

* + Age <1 year. Cautionary if age 1 to <2 years -- consider on a case-by-case basis if there is a clear LKW, evidence of ischemic stroke, and plausible mechanism such as cardioembolic)
    - Thrombolytics should not be used in young children due to limitations in extrapolating the evidence from adult stroke populations to this age group and challenges in determining the last known well.
  + Sickle cell disease in patients <18 years of age is considered a contraindication to thrombolytics due to potentially increased risk of bleeding.
  + Blood pressure: systolic blood pressure >15% above the 95th percentile for age while sitting or supine. See: <https://bsolomon.us/app/pediatric-bp/>

Other eligibility (inclusion/exclusion and cautionary, including pregnancy) criteria are the same as for adults.

# Endovascular therapy:

* + Intracranial ICA, M1 MCA segment, proximal M2 MCA horizontal segment (supplies 50% of CBF or 100mL), or basilar artery occlusion.
    - Focal cerebral arteriopathy may be considered (likely for aspiration rather than stent retriever).
  + Age ≥ 6 years. Cautionary if age 4-6 years, discuss with interventionalist.
    - EVT for children <4 years is often precluded by vessel size and complicated by increased proclivity to vessel spasm but can be considered on a case-by-case basis. Contact aspiration may be used instead of stent retriever in very young children.
  + Time window & ASPECTS:
    - Anterior circulation:
      * ASPECTS ≥ 3 within 6 hours may qualify
      * ASPECTS ≥ 6 may qualify up to 24 hours
        + If using ASPECTS score, scan should be done within 2 hours of mechanical thrombectomy
    - Posterior circulation: Up to 24 hours may quality
  + NIHSS ≥6 or [PedNIHSS](https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/10270/pediatric-nih-stroke-scale-nihss) >6 or evidence of non-improving moderate to severe stroke
  + EXCLUSIONS to endovascular therapy: Age <4 years, Moyamoya, focal cerebral arteriopathy

# Imaging:

The rate of stroke mimics is higher in children, and a hyperacute stroke protocol MRI scan is preferred to confirm acute infarction. However, if CT or CTA are done and are convincing, MRI is not required.

# Patient / Family discussion recommended (primarily for OSH physicians, if they ask):

Tenecteplase is a medication that helps dissolve blood clots. We are concerned your child may be having a stroke and so getting a clot buster may give [child’s name] a better chance of recovering without disability and getting back to activities he/she enjoys. This medication is commonly used and well-studied in adults for stroke. There are few studies on the use of TNK in children, which means we have less information about its efficacy and safety in pediatric patients compared to adults. We base our recommendations primarily based on what we know of its benefits and risks in adults with stroke. Like any medication, TNK can have side effects. The most significant risk is bleeding, which can sometimes be severe. Each child may respond differently to the medication, and it is difficult to predict the exact outcome. Given [child’s name]’s [neurologic deficits], [time since last known normal], [MRI and CTA findings], and [other factors – medical history, etc.] - we [recommend OR do not recommend] TNK be administered.

# Blood pressure parameters following thrombolytics in children:

Treat if persistent blood pressure elevation (for more than one hour) of 15% or more above the 95th percentile based on age/gender/height norms or treat immediately if elevation of or greater than 20% of the 95th percentile norms.

If provider, you are speaking to does not know how to treat pediatric blood pressure ask the transfer center to conference in the HMC Pediatric ICU attending for assistance.
